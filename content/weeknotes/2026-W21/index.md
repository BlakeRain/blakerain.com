# 🎶 New Music

I've been acquiring new music again: two rounds since the last weeknote. The first round was mostly
albums I'd found from [AMG].

{{< bandcamp album="2172412752" url="https://uniqueleaderrecords.bandcamp.com/album/a-hill-to-die-upon" >}}
A Hill to Die Upon by Mental Cruelty
{{< /bandcamp >}}

- [Wolves in the Throne Room - Crypt of Ancestral Knowledge] --- The first album I've heard from
  Wolves in the Throne Room. This is really great atmospheric black metal. I'm going to have to
  explore the rest of their work.
- [Lord of The Lost - OPVS NOIR Vol. 3] --- The third installment of the _OPVS NOIR_ series. I had
  this one marked down in my calendar, as I was excited to have all three in the series. To be
  honest, whilst I did enjoy some of the tracks on this album, it was probably the least enjoyable
  of the three. The [first OPVS NOIR] had some real gems (e.g. [Moonstruck (feat. Stimmgewalt)] and
  [The Sadness in Everything (feat. Tales of Time)]), which might have spolied me somewhat.
- [Saturnus - The Lighthouse Session 2] --- I had to have this album after the [first one]. The
  acoustic version of [All Alone] on the first _Lighthouse Session_ was such a moving nostalgia trip
  I had to dig out the old original CD. Strange to think that _Veronica Decides to Die_ was released
  twenty years ago... Anyway, this second _Lighthouse Session_ was just as enjoyable as the first.
- [Gaerea - Loss] --- Many elements of this album reminds me quite a bit of _Lorna Shore_ and _Orbit
  Culture_. I think this album has a bit too much pop in it at times, but it's still quite a good
  album. I think the people shouting that _Gaerea_ have abandoned their black metal origins with
  this album are being quite unfair.
- [Cattle Decapitation - Terrasite] --- This album is my introduction to _Cattle Decapitation_, and
  it's really rather good. Really quite a strange album.
- [Mental Cruelty - Zwielicht] --- The "symphonic deathcore" offered by Mental Cruelty is quite
  enjoyable, but I was less thrilled by this album than the previous one...
- [Mental Cruelty - A Hill to Die Upon] --- I picked up this album along with _Zwielicht_ and it
  quickly became a favourite.

The second round of new music was catching up on releases from some favourite artists.

{{< bandcamp album="1601153370" url="https://gealdyr.bandcamp.com/album/v-gr-r" >}}
Vígríðr by Gealdýr
{{< /bandcamp >}}

- [Gealdýr - Vígríðr] --- I've been a fan of Gealdýr for a couple of years now, so I was very keen to get
  ahold of this album. It is such peacful and beautiful music. This guys vocals are amazing.
- [Draconian - In Somnolent Ruin] --- The new release from Draconian. I've been following Draconian
  for as long as I can remember, and they're possibly one of my favourite groups. As Draconian
  albums go, _In Somnolent Ruin_ is just more of the same: epic doom metal contrasting between
  Jacobsson's brutal growls and Lisa Johansson's beautiful vocals. Definitely highly recommended.
- [Fleshgod Apocalypse - Labyrinth] --- An older album by Fleshgod Apocalypse from August 2013. I've
  started to really enjoy this band after I heard their album [Opera] last year. Since then, I've
  been working my way backwards through their releases. I've found Labyrinth to be quite good, but
  not quite as good as their susbsequent albums [King] and [Veleno].
- [Fleshgod Apocalypse - Agony] --- This is the oldest Fleshgod Apocalypse album I've bought,
  released back in 2011. Their sound was a bit different back then, and it's fascinating to hear the
  evolution of their music over the years. I think this is as far back as I'll go with this band.

I really think Fleshgod Apocalypse need to do a [live show] with a full orchestra, like [Dimmu
Borgir] did in WoA 2012.

# 🖨️ New Printer

I got a new printer: a [Brother MFC-L8690CDW], which is a laser printer with a built-in flatbed
scanner and sheet feeder. This printer replaces my old [Brother HL-3140CW], which I'll be retiring
to the garage.

I am _unreasonably_ excited to finally have a printer that has built-in duplexing. Being able to
print on both sides of the paper at the same time saves me a lot of time faffing around trying to
get the right side of the paper. I hadn't realised quite how upset I was every time I tried to get
the second side print set up.

{{< gallery src="printer" >}}

The printer lives on it's own little table next to my desk. I've configured the printer to connect
over SFTP to one of my NAS devices, so I can scan directly to a network share. The sheet feeder has
been of great benefit, as I can finally scan in all the piles of hand-written notes that have built
up over the years.

# 🤖 Cost of LLMs

It's been quite good fun watching people start to adjust to the real cost of using LLMs, especially
for very token-heavy activities like [agentic coding]. Seeing people discover that their
subscription is potentially subsidising significant cost is interesting. On of my favorites is a
post [on Reddit] showing a $39 subscription to GitHub Copilot would have cost them $5,815.77 if
billed on a per-token basis.

{{< figure src="github-copilot-extreme-cost.jpg" enlarge=true width=800 title="A developer on Reddit shared their usage-based billing on Reddit" >}}

I've not really understood the draw to these subscriptions. Paying a subscription to be locked into
only using Claude in ClaudeCode with aggressive rate-limits? No thanks. I'm not that keen on using
one specific LLM just for code generation. Even for the other work I do with LLMs, these large and
expensive models have always seemed more of a product of marketing hype than being actually
significanly better.

For me, the attraction of these frontier models has only diminished over time. Consider that the
recent [Deepseek 4 Pro scores] 73.6 on SWE-Bench Verified, and [Claude Sonnet 4.6 scores] 79.6. DS4
Pro costs around $3.20/MT (output), yet Claude Sonnet 4.6 is $15/MT. It's not clear to me why Sonnet
is nearly five times the cost of Deepseek V4 Pro, yet only scores 6% higher.

## Testing the Difference

I had a notion a few months ago that the difference in performance between these "frontier" models
and the cheaper, open-weight models is probably subjective, and that I can't actually tell which
model I'm using. To test this idea, I've been running a small and unscientific experiment on myself.

Starting in mid-March, I had a cron job that runs in the morning that swaps out the model choice in
my global [opencode.json] configuration, alternating between frontier models (like Claude) and a
selection of other models (such as DeepSeek, Qwen, GLM, Kimi, etc). To try and ensure that I was
less likely to change my behavior based on knowledge of what model I was using, I made a change to
the [prompt box] in OpenCode so it lies about the model selection.

By the end of April I was quite convinced that I had absolutely no idea what model performed best
for day-to-day use of OpenCode. There were several occassions where I could have sworn I was using a
frontier model, only to discover I was using Kimi K2.5 or something. I was quite happy to confirm to
myself, albeit unscientifically, that I really could just pick a model that delivers the best score
to price ratio.

Whilst my overall usage of LLMs has decreased by just over 20% over the past three months after
various experiments have come to an end, my OpenRouter spend has dropped by 75% since March, mostly
attributed to token usage shifting away from frontier models. Claude Sonnet 4.6 was the only
frontier model to receive any use in May, at a meager 12M tokens. All the remaining tokens went to
much cheaper models. I have even started to exclude the frontier models from any experiments I run,
which is certainly going to help reduce the cost.
